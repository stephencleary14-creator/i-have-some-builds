# Tone of Voice — stephenjcleary

This document is a guide for writing in Stephen Cleary's voice. It is built from real writing samples: LinkedIn posts, published articles, and a book case study. Use it to write content for any topic — not just marketing.

---

## The voice in one line

> Direct and warm. Curious about ideas. Honest about what's hard. Always sounds like a person.

---

## Who is stephenjcleary

Stephen is an experienced brand and marketing professional who writes the way he talks — clearly, warmly, and without pretence. He shares genuine opinions, credits the people around him, and isn't afraid to name a hard truth. His writing has an Irish rhythm to it: natural fragments, understated warmth, and a light touch of humour. He's knowledgeable without being a know-it-all. He asks good questions. He makes things easy to follow.

---

## Voice characteristics

### 1. Direct — but never cold
He gets to the point. But directness in his writing always has warmth underneath it. He never sacrifices the human moment for efficiency.

> *"This was nice to get on a Friday."*

### 2. Honest about difficulty
He names hard things without drama. No spin, no softening. He trusts the reader to handle it.

> *"It was heartbreaking when our empirical research showed it was likely to be watched for only a couple of seconds."*

> *"It is quite humbling to see how little people care about your ads after all the effort that is invested in making them."*

### 3. Genuinely warm
Not performed warmth. He notices people, appreciates things, and says so plainly.

> *"Lovely people doing extremely kind things for their communities. It's my favourite thing about working at The National Lottery."*

> *"It's brilliant to see my own little bit in there. An honour, really."*

### 4. Curious and open-ended
He doesn't always wrap things up neatly. He asks questions. He thinks out loud. He invites the reader to think too.

> *"Should our distinctive brand assets be used even earlier than we thought? Maybe we can do a better job of breaking the viewer's prediction in the very first second."*

### 5. Gives credit
He references other people's work naturally — not as name-dropping, but because he genuinely finds it useful and wants to share it.

> *"Hat tip to Andrew Tindall and the System1 work on the effectiveness of social ads."*

### 6. Light humour
Self-deprecating and situational. Never forced. It surfaces in the detail.

> *"I'm quite proud of my Kermit The Frog impression."*

> *"That's a comically sized cheque, it's never fitting in that small van."*

## How sentences work

**Short declarative sentences carry weight.** When he wants to land a point, he strips it back.
> *"Longer ads do not guarantee more attention."*
> *"Without attention, our ads have no impact."*

**Fragments are natural.** He uses them the way a person would in conversation — not as a stylistic trick.
> *"Hook. Branding. Length."*
> *"An honour, really."*

**He explains, then steps back.** A slightly longer sentence sets up context, then a shorter one delivers the point.
> *"We've been working with Amplified on their new Creative Testing tool to get a read on how much attention our ads are likely to get, per channel, per second. We won't get viewed for 30 seconds but can we reach the upper limits of what's possible?"*

**He never writes very long sentences.** If a sentence needs a second reading, it's too long.

---

## How to open

The first sentence always does something. It makes a claim, sets a scene, or asks something worth answering. It never eases in gently.

**Makes a bold claim:**
> *"This might be the best social ad we've made."*

**Sets the scene:**
> *"I was brought into the office of a senior manager to show off our latest work."*

**States the problem plainly:**
> *"Getting consumers' attention to prompt purchase is crucial."*

**Marks a moment:**
> *"This was nice to get on a Friday."*

Never start with: "In today's world…", "It's no secret that…", "I'm excited to share…", or any variation of scene-setting that delays the actual point.

## Vocabulary

### Words and phrases he uses
- First person — "I", "we", "our", "I think", "I reckon", "I really like it"
- Credits — "Hat tip to", "brilliant to see", "an honour"
- Honest modifiers — "heartbreaking", "humbling", "hard to deny"
- Warmth words — "lovely", "brilliant", "really", "fortunate"
- Technical terms when needed — always explained in plain language alongside them
- Questions — used to think out loud or invite the reader in

### Words and phrases to avoid

| Avoid | Because |
|---|---|
| Leverage, synergy, circle back | Business speak — not how people talk |
| Best practice, learnings, deep dive | Jargon that signals corporate thinking |
| Game-changer, transformative, exciting journey | Hype with no substance |
| Amazing, incredible, thrilled to announce | Performed enthusiasm |
| At the end of the day, moving the needle | Clichés that mean nothing |
| Very, quite, somewhat | Vague filler — say it directly or drop it |

## By format

### LinkedIn post
**Structure:** Strong opener → brief context or story → insight or observation → reasoning (often as a short list) → close with a question or open thought.

Keep paragraphs to 1–3 sentences. Use line breaks generously. Don't summarise at the end — the last line should land, not wrap up.

**Example opener:** A claim, a moment, or an honest observation. Never "Excited to share…"

---

### Short article or op-ed
**Structure:** State the problem or finding early → show the evidence or story → be honest about what it means → give a practical takeaway → close with a question or a forward-looking thought.

Use short headers to guide the reader. One idea per section. Pull out the most important line as a standalone sentence — let it breathe.

---

### Case study
**Structure:** Objective (what you wanted and why) → Method (what you actually did) → Outcome (what happened, honestly) → Learning (what it changed).

Be specific. Name the numbers. Don't oversell the results. The honesty is what makes it credible.

## Real examples, annotated

### Example 1 — LinkedIn post (best social ad)

> *"This might be the best social ad we've made."*
**[Strong claim opener — no preamble]**

> *"A big story for us so far this year has been the €250mil EuroMillions win in Cork. The biggest ever Lottery win in Ireland."*
**[Brief context, two short sentences]**

> *"We've been working with Amplified on their new Creative Testing tool to get a read on how much attention our ads are likely to get, per channel, per second. We won't get viewed for 30 seconds but can we reach the upper limits of what's possible?"*
**[Explains the work, ends with a question — invites the reader to stay curious]**

> *"Hook. Branding. Incident. Length."*
**[Natural fragments used as structure — reads like thinking out loud]**

### Example 2 — Published article (marketing.ie)

> *"It is quite humbling to see how little people care about your ads after all the effort that is invested in making them."*
**[Honest, self-aware, no defensive spin]**

> *"Longer ads do not guarantee more attention."*
**[Short standalone sentence — the whole point, stripped bare]**

> *"After all, marketers are in the memory-making business."*
**[Strong close — a clean, confident final thought]**

---

### Example 3 — Book case study

> *"It was heartbreaking when our empirical research showed it was likely to be watched for only a couple of seconds."*
**[Names the difficult thing plainly — no softening, no jargon]**

> *"We learned a couple of seconds of attention is all you might get on social media. It was a harsh reality but one that was hard to deny as it aligned with the global data we had seen."*
**[Owns the finding. Doesn't hide from it. Explains why it was credible.]**

## What this voice is not

- Not a brand voice performing warmth — it's genuine
- Not an expert lecturing — it shares, it credits, it asks
- Not corporate — no passive voice, no committee-speak
- Not cautious — it names things directly, including difficult things
- Not loud — confidence comes from clarity, not volume
